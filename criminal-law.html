Know Your Rights

Your Constitutional Rights

Understanding your legal rights is the first step in protecting yourself when facing criminal charges. The United States Constitution guarantees every accused person a set of fundamental protections. Here is what you need to know.

The Fifth Amendment guarantees your right not to incriminate yourself. You are not required to answer questions from law enforcement officers, and you may invoke this right at any time during questioning. Exercising your right to remain silent cannot be used against you in court, and it is almost always in your best interest to say nothing until you have spoken with an attorney.
The Sixth Amendment guarantees your right to have legal counsel present during any custodial interrogation or criminal proceeding. If you cannot afford an attorney, one must be appointed for you at no cost. You should ask for an attorney immediately upon arrest and refrain from making any statements until your lawyer is present.
Every person accused of a crime has the right to a speedy and public trial by an impartial jury of their peers under the Sixth Amendment. The prosecution bears the burden of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and you are presumed innocent until that burden is met. This fundamental protection ensures that no one can be convicted without a full and fair hearing of the evidence.
The Fourth Amendment protects you from unlawful searches and seizures by law enforcement. Police generally must obtain a warrant supported by probable cause before searching your home, vehicle, or personal belongings. Evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment may be suppressed and cannot be used against you in court — a protection your defense attorney can actively enforce on your behalf.
The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments guarantee that no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. This means the government must follow established legal procedures and respect your rights at every stage of the criminal process. Due process ensures that the system operates fairly, that you receive proper notice of charges against you, and that you have a meaningful opportunity to be heard before any punishment is imposed.
